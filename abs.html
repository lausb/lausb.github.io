1 Shibo Liu (Sep 24, 2025)

Schrödinger–Poisson–Slater equations with nonlinearity subscaled near zero

We discuss our results on the zero mass stationary Schrödinger–Poisson–Slater equation \[ - \Delta u + \Big ( \frac {1}{4 \pi |x|} \ast u^2 \Big ) u = \iint _\Omega f (|x|, u) \text {, \qquad } u \in \mathcal {D}^{1, 2} (\mathbb {R}^3) \text {,} \] whose time dependent counterpart comes from an approximation of the Hartree–Fock model of a quantum many-body system of electrons, in which the nonlocal convolution term represents the Coulombic repulsion between the electrons.

The local term \(f (|x|, u) = |u|^{q - 2} u\) with \(q = \frac {8}{3}\) was introduced by Slater (1951) as a local approximation of the exchange potential in the Hartree–Fock model. In the context of Thomas–Fermi–Dirac–von Weizsacker model of Density Functional Theory we will encounter local nonlinearity \(f (| x |, u) = u^{5 / 3} - u^{7 / 3}\).

A comment feature of these models is that \(f (| x |, \cdot )\) is \(3\)-sublinear (subscaled) at zero: \[ \lim _{t \rightarrow 0} \frac {f (| x |, t) t}{| t |^3} = + \infty \text {.} \] Using variational methods, for such subscaled equations:

  1. if \(f (| x |, \cdot )\) is odd without any condition on \(f (| x |, t)\) for \(| t | \gg 1\) we obtain a sequence of solutions with negative energy;
  2. if \(f \approx \lambda | t | t\) for \(| t | \gg 1\) we obtain a nontrivial solution provided \(\lambda \) is not an eigenvalue of a related scaled eigenvalue problem.

2 Ravi P. Agarwal (Sep 10, 2025)

Four Centuries Of Fermat’s Last Theorem (FLT) And Elementary Proofs Of Some Particular Cases

Imagine jotting down a claim in the margin of a book — without proof — and leaving mathematicians puzzled for nearly 400 years. That is the story of Fermat’s Last Theorem. It states that the equation \[ a^n+b^n=c^n \] has no whole number solutions when \(n\ge 3\). Although Andrew Wiles finally proved this in 1995, his proof relies on deep, modern mathematics that few outside the field can follow.

In this talk, we take a more elementary path. We begin with Pythagorean triples (the familiar case \(n=2\)) and then explore why higher powers are far trickier. Using a family of polynomials connected to the theorem, we show how to prove FLT in many cases and reduce the rest to a finite, checkable problem. Along the way, we’ll touch on history, computation, and the enduring appeal of a deceptively simple question that has inspired generations of mathematicians.
